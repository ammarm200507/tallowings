# Tallowing — Brand Guidelines

---

## 1. Who We Are

Tallowing is a wings restaurant in Glenview, IL. We make the crispiest wings in the area — bone-in, boneless, tenders, biscuit sandwiches — and everything we fry goes into 100% beef tallow. Halal certified. Hand-breaded daily.

We are not a health food restaurant. We are not a sports bar. We are a food brand that cooks the right way and lets the food speak for itself.

**One sentence:** Tallowing is the only wing spot in Glenview built around the crunch — and the reason the crunch exists is beef tallow.

---

## 2. The Promise

Before anything else, this is what Tallowing delivers:

**The crust.** A hard, audible exterior that holds up from the fryer to the table. Not soggy. Not soft after two minutes. The kind of crust that sounds right when you bite it.

**The flavor.** Clean. You taste the chicken, the marinade, the sauce — not the oil it was cooked in. The cooking medium gets out of the way.

**The bite.** 48-hour marinated, hand-breaded, fried in beef. Every step is built toward one outcome: a wing worth eating.

This is the brand promise. Every piece of copy, every photo, every caption should point back to this.

## 5. Voice & Tone

### The Voice
Short. Declarative. Like a coach who doesn't explain himself twice. We lead with the food. The ingredient earns its mention — it doesn't lead.

### Core Rules

**Do this:**
- Lead with the food experience — crust, flavor, texture.
- Use sports language naturally — not forced.
- Bring in the tallow after the food has already sold itself.
- Be proud of Glenview. Local specificity is a strength.
- Short sentences. One idea at a time.

**Never do this:**
- Don't open with "beef tallow" — open with what beef tallow produces.
- Don't use wellness language ("ancestral," "inflammatory," "gut health").
- Don't hedge ("we think," "we believe," "kind of").
- Don't lecture people about seed oils. State the fact and move on.
- Don't use generic restaurant copy ("fresh ingredients," "made with love," "family recipes").
- Don't use exclamation points more than once per page.

### Tone by Context

| Context | Tone |
|---|---|
| Headlines | Crispiness-first, punchy, declarative |
| Body copy | Food-forward, then ingredient-confident |
| Social media | Shorter, more attitude, let the food visual do the work |
| Email / announcements | Direct, local pride, FOMO-aware |
| Menu descriptions | Factual and specific — process is the selling point |

## 7. The Tallow Effect

This is the named concept that explains why Tallowing wings are different. It is not a wellness pitch. It is a cooking explanation that happens to make the food better.

### What It Is
Beef tallow fries at 400°F+ without breaking down. That high, stable heat is what creates the crust. Not the seasoning. Not the technique alone. The fat.

Most places use seed oils because they're cheap. Seed oils oxidize under heat — the crust softens, the flavor muddies, and you're tasting the oil instead of the chicken.

Tallow doesn't do that. It seals the exterior fast, locks in moisture, and gets out of the way. What you're left with is a wing that actually sounds crispy when you bite it.

**That's The Tallow Effect.**

### How to Use This Concept
- "The Tallow Effect" is a named section on the website and in brand materials.
- It always comes *after* the food has been introduced — never as the opening pitch.
- The arc is: **crispiness first → explain the why → The Tallow Effect**.
- Keep it to 3–4 sentences max when used in short-form copy. Don't expand it into a science lecture.

### Approved Short-Form Versions

**One line:**
> Beef tallow runs hot, stays stable, and builds a crust that holds. That's The Tallow Effect.

**Two lines:**
> Most places fry in seed oils. Seed oils break down under heat — soggy crust, off flavors. We use beef tallow. It runs hotter, stays cleaner, and builds a crust that actually holds. That's The Tallow Effect.

**Headline only:**
> The Tallow Effect: why our wings are crispier.

## 9. Typography

### Fonts

| Font | Weight | Usage |
|---|---|---|
| **Bebas Neue** | 400 | All headlines (H1–H3), nav items, section labels, ticker text, heat badges, countdown timer |
| **Montserrat** | 400, 500, 600, 700 | Body copy, form labels, button text, descriptions, fine print |

### Rules
- Headlines are always Bebas Neue. No exceptions.
- Body copy is always Montserrat.
- Letter-spacing on Bebas Neue headings: `0.02em` to `0.08em` depending on size — wider tracking on smaller sizes.
- Never use italic on Bebas Neue.
- Eyebrow labels (small all-caps above headlines): Montserrat 600, `0.25em` letter-spacing, gold color.

### Type Scale (approximate)
| Element | Size | Font |
|---|---|---|
| H1 Hero | `clamp(3.5rem, 10vw, 8rem)` | Bebas Neue |
| H2 Section | `clamp(2.5rem, 6vw, 5rem)` | Bebas Neue |
| H3 Card | `1.5rem – 1.8rem` | Bebas Neue |
| Eyebrow | `0.75rem` | Montserrat 600 |
| Body | `0.95rem – 1.1rem` | Montserrat 400 |
| Small / Fine print | `0.72rem – 0.8rem` | Montserrat 400 |

## 11. Menu Language

### Naming Conventions
- Sauce heat tiers: **Starter → Varsity → All-Pro** (never "mild / medium / hot")
- Menu sections: use the sports vocabulary ("The Playbook," "Sauce Depth Chart")
- Limited items get a badge, not an asterisk: `30/DAY` in gold

### Describing Food
Be specific and factual. The process is the selling point. Lead with what the customer experiences, then the method.

**Good:**
> Crispy, golden tenders. 24-hour buttermilk marinated, hand-breaded, fried in beef tallow.

**Bad:**
> Our delicious tenders are made fresh every day with the finest ingredients.

### Sauce Name Rules
Sauce names follow the sports theme — they have a name (the brand) and a descriptor (the sell).
- Name: `GARLIC POWER PLAY` (Bebas Neue, prominent)
- Descriptor: `Creamy garlic sauce finished with parmesan.` (Montserrat, subdued)

## 12. Social Media Voice

### Instagram / TikTok
- Short captions. The food visual does most of the work.
- Lead with what you see in the frame — crust, sauce, steam, texture.
- Bring in tallow only when it earns its place in the caption.
- Handle: `@tallowing`
- Tone: Confident, a little attitude, Glenview proud.

**Caption examples:**
> That crust doesn't happen by accident.

> 30 biscuit sandwiches a day. We don't make more. Get there.

> Beef tallow. That's why.

> Glenview, we're almost ready.

> The Tallow Effect: real, and coming to Waukegan Rd.

### What to Post
- Close-up food shots — crust and texture are the hero
- Behind-the-scenes prep (fryer, breading, sauce pours)
- Opening countdown content
- Local Glenview references
- Team introductions closer to opening

### What to Avoid
- Opening captions with "beef tallow" — show the food first
- Wellness / diet framing
- Overly produced content that looks like a chain
- Generic "tag a friend" posts

## 13. Photography & Visual Direction

### Style
- Dark, moody backgrounds — black or very dark surfaces
- High contrast — the golden crust against dark background
- Close crops — texture matters more than full plate shots
- Natural steam, real sauces, imperfect drizzles — no styling that looks fake

### Colors in Photography
- The gold of the fried crust is the hero of every food shot
- Red sauce pops against dark backgrounds — use it
- Avoid white plates — use dark surfaces, butcher paper, or black slate

### What to Avoid
- Bright white studio food photography
- Overly clean, too-perfect plating
- Stock photography of any kind
- Any visual that could belong to a chain restaurant
